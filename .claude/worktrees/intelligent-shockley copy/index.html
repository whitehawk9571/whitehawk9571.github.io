PARABLE OF THE PENNIES
a playable post on how fair trades make an unfair world

This is a story of how fair rules can create an unfair world.

Meet Alice and Bob. They each start with $100. They're about to play a simple trading game — just flipping a coin. No cheating, no tricks.

Here are the rules. Each round, they flip a coin. The stake is always a percentage of the poorer person's wealth:

Notice: the poorer person gains more on heads than they lose on tails. So this game is actually slightly rigged in the poorer person's favor! Surely this means wealth stays roughly equal over time, right?

Well...

click the coin a bunch of times and find out:

Huh. Even though the game is tilted in the poorer person's favor, one person keeps pulling ahead while the other keeps falling behind. And once someone gets way richer, the gap just keeps growing.

Was that just bad luck? Let's speed things up and see what happens over hundreds of flips.

hit play and run the simulation a few times:

Every time, one person ends up with almost everything. But how? The expected value favors the poorer person!

Here's the trick: when you're poor, even a small loss is a huge percentage of what you have. Losing $20 when you have $100 is devastating. Losing $20 when you have $900? Barely a dent.

This is called multiplicative dynamics. In a game where bets are proportional to your wealth, the math of percentages beats the math of expected value. The rich can absorb losses; the poor can't.

And this isn't just two people. Watch what happens with a whole society.

50 people, all starting equal. hit play:

Starting from perfect equality, with fair coin flips, you get a handful of people with almost all the wealth and everyone else with almost nothing.

The Gini coefficient (a measure of inequality where 0 = everyone equal, 1 = one person has everything) climbs relentlessly toward 1. No one cheated. No one was smarter. It's just math.

Fair rules + random luck = unfair outcomes.
That's the Yard Sale Model.

But here's the hopeful part: we can fix it.

One idea: what if we change the rules so the poorer person loses even less on a bad flip? Like, what if instead of losing 16.7%, they only lost 10%? Or 5%?

try lowering the loss fraction:

Interesting! Lowering the loss fraction slows the concentration, but doesn't stop it. Even with a tiny loss fraction, wealth still eventually piles up at the top.

The fundamental problem is multiplicative dynamics itself. As long as people are risking percentages of their wealth, the math pushes toward concentration.

So what actually works? Redistribution.

What if every round, everyone pays a tiny wealth tax — say, 1% of their wealth — and the total is split equally among everyone?

try adding a wealth tax. even a tiny one:

Whoa. Even a tiny wealth tax completely changes the outcome. Instead of one person ending up with everything, the wealth distribution stays relatively flat. The Gini coefficient stabilizes instead of climbing to 1.

The key insight: redistribution counteracts multiplicative dynamics. A small flow of wealth from the top back to the bottom is enough to prevent runaway concentration.

WRAPPING UP:

1. Fair rules can create unfair worlds.
You don't need cheating, corruption, or conspiracy to get extreme inequality. Just random luck + multiplicative dynamics is enough.

2. Inequality isn't (just) about bad actors.
The structure of the system matters as much as individual behavior. Even a game that favors the poor still concentrates wealth at the top.

3. Small redistribution makes a big difference.
A tiny wealth tax — barely noticeable to individuals — is enough to prevent runaway inequality. The math that concentrates wealth can be counteracted by a small, steady flow back to the bottom.

This interactive explainer is based on the Yard Sale Model of wealth concentration, studied by physicist Bruce Boghosian and others. The key paper is "Is Inequality Inevitable?" (Scientific American, 2019).

The model shows that even with fair or slightly progressive rules, multiplicative dynamics inevitably concentrate wealth — unless counteracted by redistribution mechanisms. For a deeper mathematical treatment, see Boghosian's "Kinetics of wealth and the Pareto law" (2014).

The format of this "playable post" was directly inspired by Nicky Case and Vi Hart's Parable of the Polygons, which uses the same interactive storytelling approach to explain segregation dynamics.
